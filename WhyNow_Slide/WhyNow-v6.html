These are the world’s most under-allocated investable markets.

India and Southeast Asia together represent ~7% of global GDP but attract less than 5% of PE/VC flows, while the US captures ~52% from ~26% of GDP. The structural underweight persists even as both markets have reached institutional scale. Today, the opportunity set exists - the capital allocation hasn’t followed.

Return dispersion is wider here, which makes manager selection materially more important.

Return dispersion in India PE is ~24 pp and in Southeast Asia PE ~15 pp, versus ~14 pp in US PE. In markets where outcomes vary this widely, selection is not a secondary consideration. It sits at the core of the strategy.

The infrastructure for LPs to access these markets systematically is still limited.

Most institutional investors outside the region do not have the local networks, data infrastructure, or region-specific diligence frameworks needed to underwrite managers here with the same level of confidence they apply in more developed markets.

The highest-impact risks in these markets are invisible to standard LP diligence.

In India and SEA, governance discipline and the reality behind value creation playbooks vary far more across managers than most LPs expect. Frameworks built for developed-market GPs don’t surface these risks until it’s too late.

A market that rewards disciplined selection and punishes passive allocation
India and Southeast Asia represent ~7% of global GDP but attract less than 5% of global PE/VC capital. The structural underweight is the widest of any major investable region - and it persists even as both markets have reached institutional depth. India now has 430+ fund managers (IVCA) and attracted $43B in 2024; Southeast Asia added $25B, a decade ago none of it exists. Today, the question is not whether to allocate - but who to back.
