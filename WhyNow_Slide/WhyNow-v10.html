Why India & Southeast Asia Now

A fundamentally different growth story - and a structural private capital opportunity
India and Southeast Asia are building economies that are digitally native, consumption-driven, and demographically compounding. These structural characteristics create a private capital opportunity set unlike any other region today.
01
Digitally Native from the Ground Up
Digital infrastructure arrived at population scale before middle-income levels - inverting the sequence that played out in every other major economy.
India's UPI handles more daily transactions than Visa - ~half of all real-time digital payments globally. 500M+ people entered the formal financial system through digital rails in under a decade.
SEA's digital economy surpassed $300B GMV in 2025 - a 7x increase in a decade. AI infrastructure alone attracted $2.3B+ in H1 2025.
Open architecture matters. India's stack (Aadhaar, UPI, Account Aggregator) is interoperable and public. SEA is building toward regional integration through the ASEAN Digital Economy Framework Agreement, covering 680M people.
This creates an investable universe of digitally native companies - in fintech, logistics, healthtech, SaaS - that could not have existed here a decade ago.
02
Consumption-Driven, Not Export-Dependent
Growth is endogenous - powered by domestic demand and a formalising economy, not external trade cycles.
India's engine is internal. Private consumption drives ~60% of GDP. Per capita income has doubled in a decade. The middle class is projected to exceed 500M by 2030 - unlocking new consumption categories at scale.
Formalisation expands the investable economy. GST, digital payments, and Aadhaar are pulling vast informal sectors into formal, financeable channels. Every business that formalises becomes bankable and investable.
Southeast Asia adds breadth and diversification. 680 million people across six high-growth economies. Indonesia, Vietnam, Philippines, and Thailand are each growing 4-5% annually. The region is increasingly consumption-led, not just an export base.
This engine is self-sustaining - resilient to trade disruption and powered by forces that are domestic, structural, and still in early innings.
03
Demographically Compounding
The workforce expands for decades, not a single cycle - getting more productive, more urban, and more connected simultaneously.
The numbers are unmatched. 2.1 billion people across India and Southeast Asia, with a combined median age of approximately 29 - the youngest major-economy workforce globally, while developed economies contract.
Dependency ratio at historic lows. India's ratio will remain at its lowest point until the 2040s - the structural condition that powered prior high-growth phases in Japan, Korea, and elsewhere. India's window is wider.
Urbanisation has decades of headroom. India at ~37%, well below the 57% global average. Each point of urban migration drives demand for housing, healthcare, financial services - and creates companies that need growth capital.
SEA adds 700M people with a median age of ~30 to the same structural story. The combined demographic tailwind extends well beyond any single fund.

These are not cyclical tailwinds. They compound across decades - driven by forces that do not reverse. Demographics, digitization, and domestic consumption reinforce each other, creating a structural growth trajectory that is both durable and still in its early chapters.

Sources: Bain India PE Report 2025, IMF, World Bank, NPCI, e-Conomy SEA 2024, UNFPA, ADB Asian Development Outlook 2025.

Why India & Southeast Asia Now

Why PE and VC are uniquely positioned to capture structural growth in India and Southeast Asia

India and Southeast Asia are not compelling simply because they are growing fast. They are compelling because growth is happening alongside digitisation, formalisation, and a maturing manager ecosystem. That creates the conditions private capital performs best in: fragmented sectors, founder-led businesses that need institutionalisation, and room for operational value creation before outcomes are priced in public markets.

The market has reached institutional depth
India PE/VC investment reached $56B in 2024 and deal count hit a decade high in 2025. Over 430 active fund managers. Buyout share has risen to 51% of PE deal value, up from 37% in 2022, reflecting maturation beyond venture. Record domestic fundraising (ChrysCapital $2.1B, Kedaara $1.7B) signals depth that is locally generated, not dependent on foreign sentiment. SEA PE rebounded 60% to $16B, with strategic buyers from Japan, Korea, and across ASEAN diversifying exit routes. A decade ago, building a diversified private-markets program here was not possible. Today, the opportunity set exists at sufficient depth.
The timing rewards allocators who build programs now
Digital infrastructure has reached population scale. India’s UPI processes ~half of global real-time payment volume. 500M+ people who were outside the formal financial system a decade ago are now bankable, insurable, and lendable. SEA’s digital economy surpassed $300B GMV in 2025, a sevenfold increase in a decade. Formalisation through GST, digital payments, and Aadhaar is pulling vast informal sectors into financeable channels and creating investable companies in fintech, healthtech, logistics, and SaaS that did not exist here before. India became Asia’s largest IPO market in 2024. Domestic capital now provides 75% of IPO funding, up from 25% three years ago. Capital formation is accelerating. The question for allocators is no longer whether to build an India and Southeast Asia program, but how quickly.
Private capital works best where growth is structural, sectors are still professionalising, and outcomes are shaped by execution more than consensus. India and Southeast Asia increasingly offer all three.

Why India & Southeast Asia Now

India brings scale and exit depth. Southeast Asia brings digitization and multi-market optionality.

India and Southeast Asia should not be viewed as one blurred “emerging markets” bucket. They are complementary. India offers domestic scale, a deeper manager base, and increasingly credible exit markets. Southeast Asia offers a faster-evolving digital economy, fragmented sectors across multiple countries, and the ability to build regional winners rather than single-market champions. Together, they form one of the most attractive private-markets regions globally.

India gives the portfolio scale and exit depth
India combines domestic demand, a large and growing manager base, and maturing realization pathways. That makes it the natural anchor market for a regional program and supports the heavier portfolio weight already reflected in the fund’s construction.
Southeast Asia adds digitization and regional optionality
Southeast Asia offers a different kind of growth: cross-border digital adoption, fragmented sectors, and businesses that can scale across multiple economies. It complements India rather than competes with it, giving the portfolio another source of company-building opportunity.
Together, the combination is stronger than either market alone
Blending India and Southeast Asia improves exposure across sectors, manager types, and exit pathways while remaining focused on a region where demographic growth, digitization, and ecosystem maturity are all moving in the right direction. That is why the strategy is built around India as the core and Southeast Asia as the complementary second engine.
